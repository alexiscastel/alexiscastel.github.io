On September 20, 2025, I raced the 10-hour Michigan Adventure Race in Ludington with my buddy Shain. It was his first adventure race, and for both of us the goal was intentionally simple: do not give up, stay composed, and be out there for the full 10 hours.

That is exactly what we did.

We completed the on-foot section, finished the kayak leg, and got through most of the bike course before the clock forced some decisions. We did not clear everything, but we made solid calls all day and stayed in it to the end. That felt like a real win.

![Before the race in Ludington, with bikes staged and plenty of uncertainty left in the day.]({{ '/files/Michigan-Adventure-Race-Ludington-2026/shain-and-I-before-race.jpg' | relative_url }})

## The Day at a Glance

- 44.25 miles
- 8:33:46 moving time
- 2,215 ft elevation gain
- 9:42 elapsed time
- First adventure race for Shain

The track tells the story pretty well. The early part of the race was slower and more technical, then the middle opened up once we got onto the bikes, and the overall route stretched from Ludington State Park down through town and back north again. It ended up being a real all-day effort, not just in distance but in the number of decisions we had to make along the way.

## Starting with the Right Goal

I think this race went well because our expectations were set correctly from the start.

Shain had never done an adventure race before, and a 10-hour format is a serious introduction. We were not chasing a podium. We were trying to stay functional for the whole day, keep moving, and avoid the kind of bad decision that turns a long race into a miserable one.

Adventure racing rewards that mindset. Fitness matters, obviously, but patience matters too. So does staying calm when a section takes longer than expected or when the course starts forcing tradeoffs.

## Trek First, Then Settle Into the Race

We handled the on-foot section first, which was a good way to ease into the day. The pace was never going to be fast in the usual running sense. It was more about reading the map, staying efficient, and not burning energy too early.

That is one of the things I enjoy most about adventure racing. It is not just about going hard. It is about knowing when to push and when to stay patient.

![Control marker in the woods during the race.]({{ '/files/Michigan-Adventure-Race-Ludington-2026/photo-race-marker.jpg' | relative_url }})

Once we got through that opening section, it felt like we had actually arrived in the race. We were moving well, still thinking clearly, and not yet paying for anything.

## Bikes, Kayak, and a Lot of Small Decisions

The bike sections were where the day really started to flow. We also ended up swapping bikes at times to keep things efficient, which was one of those practical race-day decisions that probably matters more than it sounds. It was less about one person towing the other along and more about treating the team as one unit and using what we had as well as we could.

![Shain and me on the bike section. Official race photo.]({{ '/files/Michigan-Adventure-Race-Ludington-2026/team-on-bikes.jpg' | relative_url }})

The kayak leg was another highlight. Long races feel better when the effort changes shape during the day. Trekking, biking, and paddling each stress you differently, and the switch can almost feel like a reset if you handle it well.

Nutrition mattered a lot too. We did reasonably well there. Meal bars plus glucose-heavy food kept things stable enough that we never really fell apart. That sounds basic, but in a long race, "didn't implode" is actually a meaningful accomplishment.

## Knowing What to Skip

At some point the race stops being about ambition and starts being about honesty.

We chose to skip the southern section. It had a reputation for being tougher, and by then it was clear we were not going to have enough time to do it properly and still finish the rest of the day the way we wanted. So we made the call and kept moving.

I do not see that as backing off. I see it as racing the day that was actually in front of us. One of the easiest mistakes in adventure racing is pretending the clock is not real.

![Official race photo from one of the wooded bike sections.]({{ '/files/Michigan-Adventure-Race-Ludington-2026/photo-from-back-shain.jpg' | relative_url }})

## Why This One Was So Good

This race was a blast mostly because it matched the goal so well.

What made this one so satisfying is that it felt like a real team day from start to finish. It was long enough to be hard, varied enough to stay interesting, and manageable enough that the day never became a survival march. We were outside for nearly ten hours, stayed in the game mentally, and kept solving the next problem instead of spiraling when we could not do everything.

That is a good day.

We did not empty the course, but we did exactly what we said we wanted to do before the start: stay out there for the whole day and keep racing intelligently.

![Finish-area selfie after ten hours of racing.]({{ '/files/Michigan-Adventure-Race-Ludington-2026/selfie-finish.jpg' | relative_url }})

## Takeaways

- A simple pre-race goal is sometimes the right one
- Adventure racing is as much about decisions as fitness
- Bike swaps and other small adjustments can save a surprising amount of time and energy
- Nutrition does not need to be fancy, but it does need to happen early and consistently
- Adventure racing works best when both people stay flexible and keep the ego out of it
