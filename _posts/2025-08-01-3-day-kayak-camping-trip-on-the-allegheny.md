On the weekend of July 11 to July 13, 2025, four of us spent three days paddling the Allegheny from Warren, Pennsylvania down to West Hickory. The plan was intentionally simple: sort out the shuttle, load the boats, move at river speed, and camp wherever the banks gave us a good place to stop.

It ended up being exactly the kind of trip I want to do more of. Nothing epic and nothing rushed, but still a trip that required steady effort and solid preparation.

What made that possible was preparation. I spent a lot of time ahead of the trip thinking through the shuttle, the boats, the camp setup, the food, and how to keep the whole weekend moving smoothly. None of that work was glamorous, but it paid off once we were on the water. The trip felt manageable because the logistics were not an afterthought.

![Four of us in front of the loaded truck before heading out for the trip.]({{ '/files/3-day-kayak-camping-trip-on-the-allegheny/20250711_074157.jpg' | relative_url }})

## Getting on the Water

One thing I liked about this trip is that the logistics stayed manageable. We had one canoe, two kayaks, and a route that made sense for a point-to-point weekend. Once the shuttle details were handled, the rest of the job was just packing the boats sensibly and finally getting them in the river.

We rented the kayaks from Allegheny Outfitters. For us it was close to a turnkey solution. Instead of piecing together boats, transportation, and shuttle logistics separately, we were able to lean on a setup that made the whole weekend much easier to execute.

![Boats loaded at the riverbank before we started downstream.]({{ '/files/3-day-kayak-camping-trip-on-the-allegheny/20250711_110050.jpg' | relative_url }})

After that, the trip settled into a rhythm. The Allegheny here is wide and generally calm, but it still required work. Some stretches were harder when we were paddling into the wind. Still, it never felt out of hand. The goal was to keep the effort steady, not overdo it on day one, and have enough left for all three days.

![Canoe and kayaks drifting together on a calm stretch of the Allegheny.]({{ '/files/3-day-kayak-camping-trip-on-the-allegheny/IMG_20250714_211114.jpg' | relative_url }})

## Day One: Find the Rhythm, Find Camp

The first day was mostly about finding the right pace. We stopped when something looked interesting, drifted when it made sense, and never tried to turn the weekend into a mileage contest. We were moving for most of the day, but in a way that felt sustainable.

By late afternoon we found exactly what we wanted for camp: a flat riverside spot with room for a tent, a hammock, and a fire ring.

![One of our riverbank stops during the first day downstream.]({{ '/files/3-day-kayak-camping-trip-on-the-allegheny/20250711_141142.jpg' | relative_url }})

![Camp taking shape on a wooded stretch of riverbank.]({{ '/files/3-day-kayak-camping-trip-on-the-allegheny/20250711_184832.jpg' | relative_url }})

Boats were pulled up, gear was spread out everywhere, and camp came together pretty quickly. For me, this was my first time really wild camping like this: no facilities, no electricity, just a riverbank camp and whatever we had brought with us. It was also my first night sleeping in a hammock. At some point that evening, we also had a raccoon wandering around camp and getting more attention than it deserved.

![Campfire and chairs after dark on the first night.]({{ '/files/3-day-kayak-camping-trip-on-the-allegheny/20250711_214904.jpg' | relative_url }})

## Day Two: River Time

I do not have as many photos from the middle of the trip, which is probably a good sign. Day two felt less like the start of the trip and more like the main part of it: paddle, stop, eat something, keep moving, and look for the next good place to camp.

There were still a few memorable details. A snapping turtle showed up on the bank in the morning. Later that day, we also crossed paths with what looked like some kind of river float event, with plenty of people making their way downstream in a much looser style than we were.

![Snapping turtle at the water's edge near camp.]({{ '/files/3-day-kayak-camping-trip-on-the-allegheny/20250712_073523.jpg' | relative_url }})

The other standout was dinner. At some point the trip produced a walleye on the griddle, and that is hard to improve on after a full day outside.

![Fresh fish cooking on the griddle over campfire coals.]({{ '/files/3-day-kayak-camping-trip-on-the-allegheny/20250712_181657.jpg' | relative_url }})

## The Last Morning into West Hickory

The best-looking part of the whole trip might have been the last morning. We woke up to fog hanging over the river and a camp that was still quiet before we started packing up.

![Quiet riverside camp on a foggy morning before packing up.]({{ '/files/3-day-kayak-camping-trip-on-the-allegheny/20250713_065247.jpg' | relative_url }})

![Morning fog and boats pulled up on the Allegheny before the final stretch.]({{ '/files/3-day-kayak-camping-trip-on-the-allegheny/20250713_065829.jpg' | relative_url }})

The final run into West Hickory felt like a good ending to the weekend. We walked around a bit once we got there, found a local store, bought more water, and immediately ended up with chocolate melting in our hands.

This was not a huge objective trip, and that is exactly why it worked. We were definitely tired by the end, but it never crossed over into miserable. It was three days on the Allegheny with a good crew, simple camp life, and enough steady work each day to make the weekend feel earned.

I may come back and add exact mileage or campsite notes later if I dig up a route log, but even without that, the shape of the weekend is clear: good planning, a solid launch, steady paddling, good camps, and a finish in West Hickory that wrapped the trip up well.
